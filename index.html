AI Literacy & Safety for HR

Before using AI in any HR task, understand what it can do, what it cannot do, and how to keep your team and employees protected.

Before You Use AI in HR — Safety Checklist

Complete this checklist before pasting anything into an AI tool. Click each item to check it off.

🔒 Data Safety — check before every AI session
  • I have removed all real employee names and replaced them with [Employee A], [Manager B], [Candidate C]
  • I have removed all Social Security Numbers and government-issued ID numbers
  • I have removed all medical, disability, and health information
  • I have removed specific immigration case details tied to individuals
  • I have removed payroll figures tied to specific named employees
  • I have removed confidential investigation details that could identify parties
  • I am using my organisation's approved AI tool, or I understand this is a public AI tool with no data protection guarantees
📋 Output Expectations — set before generating
  • I will treat the AI output as a first draft only — not a final document
  • I will verify any policy statements, legal references, or factual claims before using the output
  • I will have the appropriate person review the output before it reaches an employee, manager, or external party
  • I know the risk level of this task (low / medium / high) and will apply the appropriate level of review
✅
You are ready to use AI when: all safety items are checked, your data is anonymised, and you know who will review the output before it is used.

What AI Can Help With in HR

✅ Low-risk — AI does well here
  • Drafting and improving tone of routine communications
  • Summarising anonymised survey or feedback data
  • Generating structured templates and checklists
  • Explaining HR policies in plain language
  • Researching training programs, vendors, or benchmarks
  • Creating first drafts of job descriptions
  • Rewriting policy documents for clarity
  • Building onboarding or offboarding checklists
⚠️ Medium-risk — AI assists, HR decides
  • Drafting employee relations communications
  • Writing performance improvement plan language
  • Summarising anonymised exit interview themes
  • Preparing talking points for difficult conversations
  • Drafting policy update announcements
💡
The AI role in HR is to:

— Organise and structure your thinking
— Speed up drafting and formatting
— Improve language, clarity, and tone
— Summarise large amounts of text
— Generate options for you to choose from

AI does not know your company, your employee, your policy, or your legal jurisdiction. You do.
Time savings are real: HR professionals using AI report saving 3–8 hours per week on drafting, research, and documentation. That time goes back to the human judgment work only you can do.

What AI Should Not Decide in HR

🚫
AI should never be the final authority on: termination decisions, accommodation determinations, investigation outcomes, immigration status advice, medical leave decisions, discipline levels, or any matter with legal consequence. These require human judgment, legal review, and accountability.
❌ Do not rely on AI for final decisions on
  • Whether to terminate an employee
  • Whether an ADA accommodation is reasonable
  • Whether an investigation finding is substantiated
  • Whether an employee's EAD or work authorization is valid
  • Whether a situation constitutes harassment or discrimination
  • Medical leave eligibility and duration
  • Whether a PIP outcome should be termination
  • Payroll dispute resolutions with legal implications
  • Any communication that could be used as evidence in litigation
✅ What AI can do instead
  • Help you organise facts and timeline before a decision
  • Draft language for a decision you have already made
  • Identify questions you should ask or facts still missing
  • Explain what a regulation says in plain language
  • Suggest a structure for an investigation summary (human fills in facts)
  • Help prepare talking points for a conversation
  • Flag things that may need legal or compliance review

Prompt Quality — Bad, Better, and Best

The quality of your AI output is directly tied to the quality of your prompt. See the difference below.

Example: Rejection email
❌ Bad prompt

"Write a rejection email."

No role. No context. No tone. AI will produce something generic and unusable.

⚠️ Better prompt

"Write a rejection email for a candidate who didn't get the job. Be professional and kind."

Has tone but no specifics. AI will produce something polished but still generic.

✅ Best prompt

"You are an HR manager at a 200-person fintech company. Write a rejection email for a candidate who reached the final round for a Senior Product Manager role. We chose someone with more enterprise experience. Tone: warm, specific, no clichés, no 'we regret to inform you.' Leave the door open for future roles. 130 words max."

Role, context, reason, tone, constraints. AI produces something usable on the first try.

The RACTIC Formula
R — Role+ A — Action+ T — Target+ I — Information+ C — Constraints
R — Role

"You are a senior HR Business Partner at a 400-person company."

A — Action

Draft / Summarise / Analyse / Rewrite / List / Compare / Explain

T — Target

Who receives this? "A final-round candidate" / "The CHRO" / "All staff"

I — Information

Context that matters: what happened, company size, industry, relevant background

C — Constraints

Word limit, format (bullets vs prose), tone, include subject line, no jargon, give 3 versions

How to Review AI Output Before Using It

Never send AI output directly. Run through this review process first — it takes 2 minutes and prevents real problems.

Review process — in order
1
Check for assumed facts. Did AI add information you didn't provide? Remove or verify anything that wasn't in your prompt.
2
Check the tone. Is it too cold? Too casual? Does it sound human? Would you be comfortable if the employee read it?
3
Check clarity. Is the required action clear? Is the deadline stated? Would someone unfamiliar with the situation understand what to do?
4
Check for sensitive data. Did AI include information you didn't notice was in your prompt? Remove names, figures, or personal details.
5
Check against your internal policy. Does AI output match your actual company policy? If not, correct it — AI doesn't know your handbook.
6
Decide who else needs to see this. Does it require legal review? Manager approval? HR leadership sign-off? Don't skip this step on high-risk matters.
Useful iteration prompts after reviewing: "Make this warmer" / "Cut to 100 words" / "Remove the assumption about X" / "Flag what needs to be verified before sending" / "How might a frustrated employee read this?"

When to Escalate — Not Everything Belongs in AI

Escalate to HR Leadership when
  • The situation involves a potential termination
  • A formal investigation is being opened or concluded
  • An employee has filed or threatened a formal complaint
  • The situation involves a senior leader or executive
  • Any workplace violence concern is raised
Escalate to Legal / Compliance when
  • An ADA accommodation is being denied or disputed
  • An EEOC charge has been filed or threatened
  • An ICE audit or immigration enforcement action is triggered
  • A payroll dispute has legal implications
  • Any communication could be used as evidence in litigation
  • The matter involves FMLA, ADA, WARN Act, or Title VII
Escalate to Immigration Counsel when
  • An EAD has expired without a timely renewal
  • An ICE Notice of Inspection (NOI) is received
  • An employee's DACA, TPS, or other deferred action status is affected by policy changes
  • An employee's I-485 or visa petition is denied mid-employment
  • A STEM OPT employee's role changes materially

HR AI Workflows

Guided cards for 10 common HR tasks. Each card explains what AI is good for in that workflow, what to keep out, and what review is needed before using the output.

✉️
Employee Email Response
Low risk
Draft HR responses to routine employee questions and communications.
What to use AI for

Drafting tone-appropriate email responses to general employee questions about benefits, time off, policy clarifications, and routine announcements.

Keep out of AI: employee names, specific case details, salary figures, medical information, or anything from an active HR case.
You are an HR [role] at [company type]. Draft a response email to an employee who asked: "[paste anonymised question]." Our policy states: [paste relevant policy]. Tone: [helpful / clear / warm]. 100 words max. If escalation to an HR manager is needed, include a polite handoff phrase.
Before sending — review for
  • Accurate policy reference
  • Clear action or next step for the employee
  • Tone appropriate for the relationship
  • No sensitive data included
🎯
Candidate Communication
Low risk
Rejections, offer confirmations, interview invites, and status updates.
What to use AI for

Drafting clear, on-brand candidate communications that are specific and human — not generic templates.

Keep out of AI: internal hiring committee discussions, salary negotiation strategy, or any reason for rejection that could create legal exposure if disclosed.
You are a recruiter at [Company]. Write a [rejection / offer / interview invitation] email for [Candidate placeholder]. Role: [Job Title]. Key detail: [one specific, genuine observation if applicable]. Tone: [warm / professional / excited]. [Word limit]. Include subject line.
Before sending — review for
  • No inadvertent disclosure of protected-class information
  • Genuine, specific language (not generic)
  • Clear next step for the candidate
📋
Name Change Request
Low risk
Processing legal name and preferred name changes with dignity and accuracy.
What to use AI for

Drafting response emails, confirmation letters, and internal coordination checklists. Never ask why the name is changing.

Keep out of AI: actual employee name, SSN, government ID numbers, reason for the change.
You are an HR coordinator. Write a response email confirming receipt of a legal name change request. Include: documents required [ID type], systems that will be updated [HRIS, payroll, email], timeline for each update, and a warm, matter-of-fact tone. Do not ask why the change is occurring. 180 words max.
Before sending — review for
  • Warm, affirming tone
  • Correct system update timelines for your company
  • No assumption about reason for change
  • State tax withholding note if address also changed

I-9 / Work Authorization Question
High risk
Responding to employee or manager questions about EAD cards and work authorization.
🚫
High-risk workflow. Use AI only to draft language — never to make eligibility determinations. Always verify with USCIS.gov and consult immigration counsel on complex cases.
Keep out of AI: employee names, specific EAD numbers, immigration case details, or any information that could identify the individual.
You are an HR coordinator. An employee [or manager] asked: "[paste anonymised question about EAD / work authorization]." Draft a professional response that: answers the process question, does not make an eligibility determination, refers complex questions to immigration counsel, and is factually accurate based on current USCIS guidance. Note anything I should verify at uscis.gov before sending.
Before sending — review for
  • No eligibility determination made by AI
  • Current USCIS rules verified
  • Immigration counsel reviewed if complex
  • No employee identifying information

Policy Explanation
Low risk
Translating complex HR policies into plain, understandable language for employees.
What to use AI for

Rewriting dense policy language into plain English, creating FAQ versions, and drafting policy announcement emails.

Keep out of AI: legal advice, jurisdiction-specific compliance determinations, or any statement that could be read as a legal guarantee.
Rewrite the following HR policy section in plain English for employees. Do not change the meaning or add information not in the original. If any section is ambiguous or could be misread, flag it in [brackets]. Audience: general employees. Tone: clear, approachable, not legalistic. [Paste policy section here — remove any employee-specific references]
Before sending — review for
  • Meaning unchanged from original policy
  • No legal interpretation added
  • Flagged ambiguities reviewed by HR or legal

Employee Complaint Summary
High risk
Organising facts and structuring complaint summaries for HR investigations.
🚫
High-risk workflow. AI can help structure and neutralise language — but never determine findings, assign fault, or make legal conclusions. Legal review required before any formal documentation is used.
Keep out of AI: real names (use [Employee A], [Manager B]), specific case numbers, or information that could identify parties if the summary were shared.
Help me structure a neutral, factual summary of the following workplace complaint notes. Use placeholders [Employee A], [Manager B] throughout. Organise as: 1) Nature of complaint. 2) Timeline of events (factual only). 3) Parties involved (placeholder names only). 4) Evidence or documentation referenced. 5) Questions still to be clarified. Flag any language that sounds like a conclusion rather than a fact so I can correct it. [Paste anonymised notes]
Before using — review for
  • All names replaced with placeholders
  • No conclusions or fault language
  • Factual statements only
  • Legal counsel reviewed before formal use

Performance Documentation
Medium risk
Drafting PIPs, performance review language, and coaching notes.
What to use AI for

Structuring clear, specific, and fair performance documentation using the SBI (Situation–Behaviour–Impact) framework. AI organises the language — you provide the facts.

Keep out of AI: employee names, salary information, or any detail that could reveal the identity of the employee if the output were shared.
You are an HR Business Partner. Help me draft neutral, factual performance documentation for [Employee placeholder] using the SBI framework (Situation, Behaviour, Impact). Facts I am providing: [describe the situation using placeholders]. Tone: professional, factual, not punitive. Flag any language that sounds like an opinion rather than an observed fact. Do not add performance conclusions I have not stated.
Before using — review for
  • Facts verified and accurate
  • No assumptions or AI-added conclusions
  • Manager and HR reviewed
  • Legal reviewed if termination is possible outcome
🎓
Training Plan
Low risk
Building L&D plans, learning objectives, and program outlines.
What to use AI for

Structuring training programs, writing Bloom's Taxonomy learning objectives, creating timelines, and building rollout communication sequences.

Keep out of AI: individual employee performance records or any information linking a specific person to a training need.
You are an L&D specialist. Build a training plan for [topic] for [audience / level]. Business goal: [goal]. Format: phased plan with learning objectives (using Bloom's Taxonomy), delivery format recommendation, timeline, and success metrics. Note any assumptions I should verify before finalising.
Before using — review for
  • Learning objectives are measurable
  • Timeline is realistic for your organisation
  • Delivery format matches your LMS/tools
📊
Data / Report Summary
Medium risk
Turning HR data into narrative summaries for leadership.
What to use AI for

Summarising anonymised survey results, turnover data, or headcount tables into readable leadership summaries with recommended actions.

Keep out of AI: individual-level data, named employees, salary details tied to specific people, or unmasked demographic breakdowns.
Here is anonymised HR data from [source — survey / turnover report / headcount table]: [paste data — anonymised]. Summarise as a 1-page leadership report including: key findings, patterns to note, 3 recommended HR actions, and any data limitations. Audience: CHRO / leadership team. Flag any finding where the sample size is too small to draw reliable conclusions.
Before using — review for
  • Data verified as anonymised before pasting
  • Findings are accurate to the data (AI can misread tables)
  • Recommendations are feasible for your organisation
🤝
Manager Coaching Script
Medium risk
Preparing managers for difficult conversations with employees.
What to use AI for

Drafting talking points, anticipated responses, and conversation frameworks so managers are prepared without being scripted.

Keep out of AI: employee names, performance ratings, disciplinary history, or any data that identifies the individual.
You are an HR Business Partner. Help me prepare a manager for a difficult conversation with [Employee placeholder] about [describe the situation with placeholders — e.g. recurring lateness, performance concern, behaviour issue]. Prepare: 1) Opening statement (direct, not confrontational). 2) Key points using SBI (Situation, Behaviour, Impact). 3) Anticipated responses and how to handle each. 4) A closing that sets clear expectations. 5) What NOT to say. Tone: calm, fair, professional.
Before using — review for
  • Facts provided are accurate
  • Tone is fair to both parties
  • Manager reviewed and agrees with framing
  • HR is looped in before the conversation

  • No real employee names or identifiable details are in the output
  • No sensitive data (SSN, medical, salary, immigration status) is included

  • There is no language that could be perceived as biased, discriminatory, or unfair
Section 5 — Review & Approval
  • This output does not require legal or compliance review before use
  • This output does not require manager or HR leadership approval before sending
  • I have personalised the output so it does not read as a generic AI template
  • I am confident this output is ready to use as-is or with minor edits

Distressed or upset employee arriving at HR
Write a reception script for handling an employee who arrives at the HR office visibly upset or distressed. Process Guide

Handling an Expired or Expiring EAD

What to do — and what not to do — when an employee's EAD is expiring, has expired, or when an automatic extension applies.

⚠️
Do not terminate without checking automatic extension eligibility. Many EAD categories qualify for automatic extensions of 180–540 days if a timely renewal was filed. Terminating an employee whose EAD expired but who filed a timely renewal could expose the company to discrimination liability. Check first. Call immigration counsel.
Scenario A
EAD is expiring in 90 days or less
Proactive reverification — best practice
  • Send reverification reminder email at 90 days and again at 30 days before expiration
  • Ask the employee to present their renewed EAD before the current one expires
  • If they've filed a timely renewal: ask them to bring the I-797C receipt notice so you can note the auto-extension in I-9 Section 3
  • Update I-9 Section 3 when the new EAD arrives — do not start a new I-9
Scenario B
EAD expired — employee has I-797C receipt showing timely renewal
Automatic extension applies — do not terminate
  • Confirm the I-797C receipt shows the same EAD category as the expired card
  • Calculate the auto-extension period: 180 days (most categories) or 540 days (C33 DACA, C08 asylum, C22 H-4) from the EAD expiration date
  • Update I-9 Section 3: note the receipt number, describe the category, and record the extension end date (EAD expiration + 180/540 days)
  • Continue employment — the employee is authorized to work
  • Set a new reverification reminder for the end of the auto-extension period
Scenario C
EAD expired — no renewal filed, no auto-extension
Unauthorized employment — must take action
⚠️
This is the only scenario where employment must cease. Allowing an employee to work without valid work authorization exposes the company to civil and criminal penalties. Consult immigration counsel before taking any action.
  • Immediately consult immigration counsel — do not act unilaterally
  • Place the employee on unpaid leave (not termination) while you verify the situation with counsel
  • Work with counsel to determine whether the employee can file an emergency EAD renewal or has other authorization options
  • Document every step taken
  • Do not terminate immediately without consulting counsel — the situation may be salvageable
  • Do not ask the employee why their EAD lapsed or about their underlying immigration case
  • Do not allow the employee to continue working without authorization
